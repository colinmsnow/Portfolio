A Boat Designed to Tip

Math, Naval Architecture

As part of a project in my Quantitative Engineering Analysis class, we were tasked to create a model boat that had very specific properties:

  • It must float flat when put in the water
  • It must right itself when tipped to less than 140 degrees, and tip completely over when tipped past this point
  • It must be as fast as possible

From here, the problem was completely left up to us to solve. Our resulting design was determined by a combination of multivariable calculus simulation and computational fluid dynamics, ensuring that it would be both stable and flat on the water.

Note: This project has a full report that can be viewed here for more of the technical details.

The Tipping Model

The basic idea behind making a boat stable involves ensuring that the forces of gravity and buoyancy are restoring, meaning that when the boat tips over these forces act to tip it back. The force of gravity is easy to calculate as it is only dependent on the boats mass and center of mass, but the force of buoyancy is somewhat more complicated as its center is at the center of mass of the water that the boat displaces, a position that moves when the boat tips. To solve this problem, we created a Mathematica script which would solve for the magnitude of the restoring force at each angle both to the side and to the front and back and created a surface of restoring magnitude versus angle. The equilibrium point, where this surface passed through zero, is the point at which it will flip completely over. By adjusting the weight in the boat until this was equal to 140 degrees, we made a boat that had exactly the stability that we wanted.

Fluid Dynamics

Once we knew how to analyze the tipping of our boat, we concentrated on making it fast. We used Autodesk CFD to run a free surface simulation of it moving through the water. Through these results we were able to make it significantly more hydrodynamic without affecting its stability

Construction

We now had a completely designed boat and it was time to test it. We laser cut the pieces out of fiberboard and assembled them precidely to get the shape we wanted. We covered it in plastic and then added lead balls to the keel to give it the right weight characteristics. We put it in the water and it worked exactly as we intended. It was stable exactly until 140 degrees, and went an incredible 1.3 m/s when pulled with a 20 gram mass.
